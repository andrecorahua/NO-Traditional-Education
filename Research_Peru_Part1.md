> **Executive** **Summary**
>
> This report provides a data-driven economic analysis of the
> traditional university system in Peru, spe‐ cifically evaluating its
> return on investment (ROI) for high-growth technology entrepreneurs.
> The ana‐ lysis is based on verifiable statistics from Peruvian and
> international sources, focusing on the quantifi‐ able costs, returns,
> and time investments associated with pursuing a university degree. The
> findings indicate that the traditional university path presents a
> complex and often suboptimal economic pro‐ position for individuals
> aiming to create high-growth technology ventures. The direct financial
> costs are substantial and highly variable, ranging from tens of
> thousands to **over** **S/** **200,000** **for** **a** **five-year**
> **program** **\[5\]**, frequently necessitating significant household
> indebtedness in a population with limited financial literacy **\[27,**
> **29\]**. Concurrently, the measurable ROI for higher education has
> been in decline, with the income premium for each additional year of
> university study diminishing over the last two decades **\[11\]**. A
> significant **portion** **of** **graduates**, approximately
> **one-third**, find themselves in positions that do not require their
> level of education, a phenomenon termed “**overeducation**” that
> severely im‐ pacts the financial viability of their educational
> investment **\[11**\]. Furthermore, **the** **theoretical**
> **five-year** **duration** **of** **a** **degree** **program** **is**
> **frequently** **a** **misrepresentation** **of** **reality**;
> empirical evidence from Peru indicates that many students remain
> enrolled beyond the prescribed timeframe, extending the period of
> foregone income and delayed participation in the labor market
> **\[58\]**. **For** **a** **technology** **entrepreneur,** **this**
> **extended** **time** **represents** **a** **critical**
> **opportunity** **cost**, delaying mar‐ ket entry, product
> development, and the acquisition of practical, industry-specific
> experience in a sec‐ tor where **speed** **is** **a** **primary**
> **determinant** **of** **success**. When contrasted with the
> potential, albeit high- risk, income trajectory of a technology
> venture, the traditional university path’s fixed, and often mod‐ est,
> income potential for graduates presents a stark economic trade-off.
> This report **concludes** that the combination of high direct costs,
> declining financial returns, extended time commitment, and im‐ mense
> opportunity cost makes the traditional university system **a**
> **questionable** **allocation** **of** **capital** **and** **time**
> **for** **aspiring** **high-growth** **technology** **entrepreneurs**
> **in** **the** **current** **Peruvian** **economic** **land‐**

> **Public** **vs.** **Private** **University** **Cost** **Structures**
>
> The Peruvian higher education system is characterized by a stark
> dichotomy in its cost structure between public and private
> institutions. Public universities, which receive a significant portion
> of their funding from the state, do not charge monthly tuition fees
> **\[3\]**. The budget allocated to public universities reached a
> record S/ 6,968 million in 2024, reflecting a substantial state
> subsidy **\[7\]**. However, attendance is not
>
> without cost. Prospective students mustbear the expense ofadmission
> exams,which can range from **S/** **300** **to** **S/** **870** at
> public universities in Lima,with fees varying by institution and, in
> some cases,by the student’s secondary school oforigin **\[6\]**.
> Additional administrative fees for admission certificates and medical
> examinations are also re‐ quired. While these direct costs are minimal
> compared to the private sector, they represent a non- trivial barrier
> for individuals from the lowest socioeconomic strata.
>
> In contrast, the private university sector operates on a commercial
> basis, with costs that span an ex‐ ceptionally wide spectrum. The
> total investment for a standard five-year career can range from ap‐
> proximately S/ 23,000 at the most affordable licensed institutions to
> well over S/ 200,000 at the most prestigious ones **\[1,** **5\]**. At
> the lower end, universities such as Universidad María Auxiliadora
> (UMA) and Universidad de Ciencias y Humanidades (UCH) offer monthly
> fees starting from S/ 460 and S/ 470, re‐ spectively **\[1\]**. In the
> mid-range, institutions like Universidad San Ignacio de Loyola (USIL)
> and Univer‐ sidad San Martín de Porres (USMP) have monthly fees that
> can extend up to S/ 3,265 and S/ 3,230 **\[10\]**. At the highest
> tier, elite institutions such as the Pontificia Universidad Católica
> del Perú (PUCP) and the Universidad del P ac ífic o (UP) command the
> highest fees. The PUCP’s monthly payments can reach S/ 4,130, leading
> to a total career investment of up to S/ 125,200 **\[4\]**. The
> Universidad del Pacífico calculates fees per credit, with a 20-credit
> cycle potentially costing up to S/ 26,220, and monthly payments
> reaching S/ 5,244 in the highest bracket **\[8\]**. For particularly
> demanding and re‐ source-intensive careers like Medicine, the costs
> escalate further. At the Universidad Peruana Cayetano Heredia (UPCH),
> annual costs for a medical degree can reach as high as S/ 74,400,
> pushing the total investment for the seven-year program well beyond
> that of a standard five-year degree **\[9\]**. This vast range in
> pricing reflects a highly segmented market where institutional
> prestige, infrastruc‐ ture, and perceived quality are directly
> correlated with the financial burden placed upon the student and their
> family.

> **Student** **and** **Household** **Indebtedness**
>
> The substantial cost of private university education in Peru directly
> contributes to a growing trend of youth and household indebtedness.
> While specific, granular national statistics on student-loan debt are
> not readily available from the Instituto Nacional de Estadística e
> Informática (INEI), broader eco‐ nomic indicators paint a clear
> picture of increasing financial strain. A 2020 study revealed that
> over 30% of young Peruvians between the ages of 18 and 24 held active
> debts, with credit cards being a primary instrument **\[22\]**. This
> financial pressure is compounded by a challenging labor market for
> youth, where unemployment rates have been among the highest in the
> region, forcing many to rely on credit to cover essential expenses,
> including education **\[22\]**.
>
> This trend is situated within a larger context of rising household
> debt across the country. Reports indic‐ ate that overall household
> debt has reached historical highs, with credit quotas consuming up to
> 32% of personal income, a sharp increase from the pre-pandemic level
> of 24% **\[29\]**. The Central Reserve Bank of Peru (BCRP) has noted
> significant expansion in credit to individuals, particularly in
> consumer loans and credit card debt, occurring even during periods of
> economic recession and declining real wages **\[30\]**. A critical
> contributing factor to this precarious situation is a widespread lack
> of financial education. According to the Superintendencia de Banca,
> Seguros y AFP (SBS), a staggering 41% of the Peruvian population has a
> low or non-existent level of financial education **\[27\]**. This
> deficiency severely hampers the ability of families to make sound
> long-term investment decisions, such as financing a uni‐ versity
> education, and increases the risk of accumulating unmanageable debt.
> For a family considering an investment in a private university
> education costing upwards of S/ 100,000, the decision often in‐ volves
> taking on significant financial leverage, a risk that is magnified by
> the uncertain ROI of the degree itself.

> **Return** **on** **Investment** **and** **Income** **Potential**
>
> The decision to invest in a university education is, from a purely
> economic standpoint, predicated on the expectation of a positive
> return. This return is typically measured by the increased income
> poten‐ tial and enhanced career opportunities afforded to graduates.
> However, recent data for Peru indicates that this return is not
> guaranteed and has, in fact, been diminishing over time, challenging
> the long-held assumption that a university degree is a reliable
> pathway to financial prosperity.
>
> **Declining** **Returns** **on** **Higher** **Education**
>
> The financial return on investment in higher education in Peru has
> experienced a notable decline over the past 15 years. A comprehensive
> study by Gustavo Yamada and Pablo Lavada quantifies this trend,
> revealing that the income premium associated with additional years of
> university study has eroded **\[11\]**. In 2004, an extra year of
> study at a private university was associated with a 22% increase in
> in‐ come compared to an individual with only a secondary education. By
> 2019, this premium had fallen to 18% **\[11\]**. A similar downward
> trend was observed for public universities, where the income premium
> decreased from 18% to 15% over the same period **\[11\]**. While these
> rates of return may still be com‐ parable to some alternative
> financial investments, their trajectory is unequivocally negative.
>
> Most critically, the study found that only 62% of university careers
> in Peru generate a positive net fin‐ ancial return, meaning that for a
> substantial 38% of graduates, the increased lifetime earnings do not
> suffice to cover the direct and indirect costs of their education
> **\[11\]**. This statistic fundamentally under‐ mines the premise of
> university as a guaranteed economic benefit. In stark contrast to the
> declining returns for university graduates, workers with technical
> education have seen their salaries increase, suggesting a market shift
> that values specific, applied skills **\[11\]**. This data presents a
> severe chal‐ lenge to the conventional wisdom, indicating that a
> significant portion of university attendees are making a financially
> irrational investment, at least when measured by direct income-based
> ROI.
>
> **The** **“Overeducation”** **Phenomenon** **and** **Institutional**
> **Quality**
>
> A primary factor contributing to the declining ROI is the phenomenon
> of “overeducation,” where a sig‐ nificant portion of university
> graduates are employed in occupations that do not require a higher
> edu‐ cation degree. Data indicates that approximately one-third of
> Peruvian university graduates fall into this category **\[11\]**. This
> mismatch is not evenly distributed across fields, being most prevalent
> in business and economics (54% of graduates) and humanities (35%), but
> also significantly affecting engin‐ eering (33%) and law (29%)
> **\[11\]**. This situation suggests a substantial misalignment between
>
> the skills supplied by the university system and the demands of the
> labor market. It also implies that many graduates are unable to
> monetize their educational credentials, effectively nullifying the
>
> expected financial return.
>
> The issue is closely linked to the rapid, and largely unregulated,
> expansion of the private university sector since the late 1990s
> **\[11\]**. This proliferation led to a dramatic increase in
> enrollment but also in‐ troduced wide variability in institutional
> quality. The implementation of a licensing process by SUNEDU has
> attempted to establish a quality baseline, and the data shows a clear
> financial penalty for attend‐ ing a lower-quality institution. In
> 2019, graduates from universities that failed to obtain SUNEDU
> licensing earned, on average, 20% less than their counterparts from
> licensed institutions. This gap widened to 25% by 2021, providing
> empirical evidence that the choice of institution has a direct and
> significant impact on a graduate’s earning potential and,
> consequently, on the ROI of their educational investment **\[11\]**.

> **Comparative** **Income** **Analysis:** **Graduate** **vs.**
> **Entrepreneur**
>
> A direct, statistically robust comparison between the income of a
> typical university graduate and a high-growth technology entrepreneur
> is inherently difficult due to the vastly different nature of their
> respective paths. However, an analysis of available data allows for a
> compelling juxtaposition of their economic prospects. The average
> monthly income from work in Peru was S/ 2,054.9 in the third quarter
> of 2024 **\[81\]**. For the formally employed, a category where
> university graduates are more likely to be found, the average salary
> was higher, at S/ 3,040 per month **\[104\]**. This figure represents
> a relatively stable, predictable, but ultimately capped income stream
> that a graduate can expect.
>
> The entrepreneurial landscape in Peru presents a starkly different
> risk-reward profile. The country has a high rate of enterprise
> creation, but the vast majority are micro and small enterprises
> (MYPEs) with moderate incomes and high failure rates; data suggests
> only one in ten new businesses survives beyond its first year
> **\[63\]**. However, for the technology entrepreneur, the potential
> upside is orders of magnitude greater. While specific income data for
> tech founders is not available, proxy indicators are revealing. Formal
> entrepreneurs in the commerce and services sectors reported average
> monthly sales of S/ 22,000 in 2021 **\[67\]**. More significantly,
> studies show that MYPEs engaging in *technological* *diversification*
> exhibit higher export values, directly linking technological
> innovation to greater revenue potential **\[64\]**. The
> entrepreneurial path is one of high risk and high variance; the
> probability of failure
>
> is significant, but the potential for wealth creation far exceeds the
> linear, salary-based progression of a traditional employee. For the
> aspiring technology founder, the economic calculation is not about
> securing an average salary, but about the possibility of achieving
> exponential growth, a possibility that the traditional employment path
> for graduates does not offer.
>
> **The** **Investment** **of** **Time** **and** **Control**

> Beyond direct financial costs, a university education demands a
> substantial investment of time. This temporal investment carries its
> own set of costs and trade-offs, particularly concerning the loss of
> autonomy and the delay in entering a chosen field. For a technology
> entrepreneur operating in a fast-paced industry, the management and
> valuation of time are critical strategic considerations.

> **Theoretical** **vs.** **Real** **Time** **to** **Graduation**
>
> The standard academic framework in Peru outlines a bachelor’s degree
> as a five-year program, typic‐ ally comprising ten academic semesters
> **\[55\]**. This theoretical duration is the basis upon which most
> prospective students and their families calculate the total cost and
> time commitment. However, empiricalevidence and systemic analysis
> suggest that this five-year timeline is more ofan ideal than a reality
> for a large segment ofthe student population. Observed trajectories
> frequently extend beyond the prescribed period, with students
> remaining enrolled for additional years beyond the theoretical
> duration **\[58\]**.
>
> This discrepancy is driven by a confluence ofsocioeconomic factors.
> Among the 8.2% ofPeruvian university students who have interrupted
> their studies, 28.5% cited a lack offinancial resources as the primary
> cause, a situation exacerbated by a low average monthly per capita
> household income ofonly S/ 991 **\[58\]**. Furthermore, many students
> have significant family care responsibilities, with 10.4% of those who
> interrupted their studies citing family-related reasons,such as the
> care ofchildren or elderly relatives. This reality conflicts with
> university structures that often fail to account for the socioeconomic
> pressures and time constraints faced by students, particularly given
> that the lack oftime is the second most reported reason for academic
> disruption **\[58\]**. This model is fundamentally incompatible with
> the reality ofa student who must also hold a job. As a result,
>
> s tu dents are often forced to take a reduced course load each
> semester, inevitably prolonging their time to graduation. This
> extension of the study period has a compounding negative effect on the
> overall economic equation. It not only increases the total direct cost
> through additional matriculation fees but, more critically, it
> lengthens the period of foregone income and delays the start of a
> professional career, dramatically increasing the opportunity cost of
> the degree.

> **Autonomy** **and** **Flexibility:** **Employee** **vs.**
> **Entrepreneur**
>
> The choice between a traditional career path post-graduation and an
> entrepreneurial one is also a choice between two fundamentally
> different paradigms of personal and professional control. The standard
> employment model in Peru is governed by a legal framework that defines
> a maximum work week of 48 hours **\[85\]**. While modern workplaces
> are increasingly adopting flexible arrangements such as hybrid models
> and objective-based work, the employee’s autonomy remains
> circumscribed **\[82\]**. Key strategic decisions, work priorities,
> and ultimate control over one’s time are dictated by the employer.
> Employment offers a degree of security, a predictable schedule, and
> benefits such as paid vacations and health insurance, **but** **it**
> **comes** **at** **the** **cost** **of** **surrendering** **a**
> **significant** **measure** **of** **personal** **sovereignty**
> **over** **one’s** **professional** **life**.
>
> **Entrepreneurship**, in contrast, offers the potential for complete
> autonomy. The founder dictates the vision, sets the priorities, and
> has total control over the allocation of their time and energy. This
> freedom is a core attraction of the entrepreneurial path **\[90\]**.
> However, this autonomy is coupled with total responsibility and
> immense risk. **The** **distinction** between work and life often
> dissolves, especially in the early stages of a venture, and the
> stability and benefits of traditional employment are absent. The
> entrepreneur bears the full weight of the enterprise’s success or
> failure. For a high-growth technology founder, the value of this
> autonomy can be immense. It allows for the agility to pivot, the speed
> to seize market opportunities, and the singular focus required to
> build a company from the ground up.
>
> The trade-off is clear: the structured, lower-risk, but constrained
> environment of employment versus the unstructured, high-risk, but
> potentially limitless environment of entrepreneurship. The decision
> hinges on an individual’s tolerance for risk and their valuation of
> control over their own time and destiny **\[90\]**.

> **Alternative** **Skill** **Acquisition** **Pathways**
>
> The traditional university system’s value proposition is predicated on
> its role as the primary conduit for acquiring advanced knowledge and
> skills. However, the rise of alternative educational models challenges
> this monopoly, particularly in the technology sector. The skills
> required to build a successful technology company—such as coding,
> digital marketing, product management, and user experience design—are
> often best learned through focused, practical application rather than
> broad, theoretical study. **Online** **learning** **platforms**,
> intensive coding bootcamps, and specialized certification programs
> offer pathways to acquire these specific, in-demand skills in a
> fraction of the time and at a fraction of the cost of a traditional
> degree. These alternatives allow an aspiring entrepreneur to rapidly
> tool up with the necessary competencies and immediately apply them to
> a business idea. While a university
>
> degree may offer a broader intellectual foundation, its slow pace and
> often outdated curriculum can be a significant disadvantage. The
> opportunity cost analysis must therefore include a comparison to these
> more agile, cost-effective, and targeted methods of skill acquisition,
> which may offer a more direct and efficient route to entrepreneurial
> readiness.

> **Conclusion**
>
> A rigorous, data-driven analysis of the traditional university system
> in Peru reveals a challenging economic proposition for the aspiring
> high-growth technology entrepreneur. The direct financial investment
> is substantial, with costs at private institutions creating a
> significant barrier and often leading to considerable household debt
> **\[29\]**. This high initial outlay is juxtaposed with a measurable
> and persistent decline in the return on investment, as evidenced by
> the shrinking income premium for graduates and the high prevalence of
> “overeducation” **\[11\]**. The data indicates that for a significant
> minority of students, the university path constitutes a negative net
> financial investment.
>
> Furthermore, the investment of time is frequently and severely
> underestimated. The reality of balancing work and study extends the
> time to graduation far beyond the theoretical five years, amplifying
> the already immense opportunity cost **\[58\]**. For a technology
> founder, this lost time is the most critical deficit, representing
> years of forgone market experience, product development, network
> building, and practical skill acquisition in an industry where
> velocity is a key determinant of success. The structured,
> authority-driven environment of academia and subsequent employment
> offers stability but stands in stark contrast to the autonomy and
> agility required for entrepreneurship. When the quantifiable costs of
